# FDB 2026 — Design Brief

A brief for the design and concept phase. Written for the Claude context, design partner, or DTTT designer who picks up the visual development of Future. Destination. Brand. 2026 from here.

This brief does not specify what the design should be. It specifies what the design needs to do, the constraints it must hold inside, and the questions it should resolve.

---

## 1. What this brief is for

The structural and content work for FDB 2026 is complete. Six pages of working HTML exist that hold:

- The right structural shape across the event
- Working copy in the FDB editorial voice for every page
- Correct application of the FDB design system tokens, types and components

What the existing pages do not do:

- Distinguish the three days as three different visual moments
- Use the stack-square motif as a flexed signature gesture across the event
- Incorporate imagery, portrait treatment, or any photographic language
- Express the journey through the event as a designed experience
- Make the X. Awards feel like a sub-brand moment rather than a section heading
- Capture the emotional register specific to each room

The design phase exists to resolve this. Its output should be: a developed visual language for the event, in which Day One reads differently from Day Two reads differently from Day Three, while all three are clearly held inside one identity.

The existing HTML build is a useful structural reference. It is not a constraint on what the design should look like.

---

## 2. The event in brief

**Name.** Future. Destination. Brand. 2026. The full mark always carries the three full stops.

**Dates.** 02 to 04 December 2026.

**Location.** Dublin, Ireland.

**Host.** Fáilte Ireland, the national tourism development authority of Ireland.

**Wider context.** The event is convened under Ireland's Presidency of the Council of the European Union (H2 2026).

**Convenor.** Digital Tourism Think Tank (DTTT), the international member network for destination marketing organisations.

**Capacity.** 30 to 40 at the leadership meeting on Day One. 125 to 200 at the keynote programme on Day Two. Day Three is members only and capped by table.

**Structure.** Three days, three rooms, one conversation:

- Day One. Leadership in the Age of AI. Invite-only boardroom meeting plus the X. Awards in the evening.
- Day Two. The keynote programme. Back-to-back keynotes from leading destinations.
- Day Three. The trends workstream. Members-only working day.

---

## 3. What makes FDB different

This section exists so the design does not default to category conventions. Most destination industry events are political, sponsor-heavy, panel-led and short on substance. FDB is built to be the opposite. The design should reflect that posture.

**No paid speakers.** No keynote fees. The room is the asset.

**No vendors, no sponsor halls, no commercial agenda.** The X. Awards has its own evening; the rest of the event has no commercial layer.

**No tokenistic interaction.** No Slido polls, no live word clouds, no audience response gimmicks.

**Editorial, not promotional.** The voice is restrained, declarative, written like a memo to a knowledgeable peer rather than a conference landing page.

**Peer authority over outside expertise.** The most valuable voices are destination marketers actively doing the work. Outside speakers expand the frame; they do not fill time.

**Policy reach as part of the design intent.** Day One produces documents written to be read in Brussels, in national capitals, and in the strategy teams of the technology companies whose decisions shape destination work. The visual treatment of those documents is part of the system, not an afterthought.

**Small by design.** The Leadership Day room holds 30 to 40 people. This is a stated choice, not a constraint. The design should not try to make a small room look bigger than it is.

## 4. The three days, their character, and the journey

Each day has its own emotional register. The design should make that legible without breaking the system.

### Day One. Leadership in the Age of AI

**Tuesday 02 December, daytime. Invite-only boardroom format. 30 to 40 senior leaders.**

The room constitutes the Global Tourism Council for AI Governance and Leadership. It issues the Dublin Declaration on AI in Tourism. It establishes the Global Tourism Code of Practice on AI. The outputs are submitted to EU institutions under the Irish Presidency.

**Atmosphere.** Close, considered, almost private. Boardroom arrangement, no stage, no panels. Held under the Chatham House Rule. The day reads like a working session of senior peers, not a programme of speakers.

**Visual cues the design should consider.** Restraint. Gravitas. A sense that what happens here travels further than the room. The treatment of the published outputs (Declaration, Code, State of Destination AI Report, Executive Briefing) is part of this day's design.

**Sub-page.** An EU AI Act companion briefing that exists as a public-facing companion to Day One. Carries a fifteen-question readiness assessment that participants complete before they arrive. Tonally aligned with Day One.

### Day One Evening. X. Awards

**Tuesday 02 December, evening. Awards format. 125 to 200 attendees.**

The first AI-Positive Industry Awards. Eight categories. A submission, shortlisting and judging process that is explicit about how AI takes part in the work being recognised. Final judging is human-only. Reasoning notes are published with every winner.

**Atmosphere.** Celebratory but considered. No black tie. Informal in atmosphere, serious in recognition. The sub-brand has its own lockup, its own roundel, its own moment.

**Visual cues the design should consider.** This is the only point in the event where the dark treatment may earn a place (the design system marks dark as a selective alternate for evening and X. Awards moments). The X. Awards lockup and mark are canonical assets and must be used unaltered. The relationship between the FDB stack-square motif and the X. Awards italic *x.* glyph is a sub-brand expression worth exploring.

### Day Two. The Keynote Programme

**Wednesday 03 December, full day. Conference format. 125 to 200 delegates.**

A packed day of back-to-back keynotes from destination marketers, brand leads and digital teams. Six themes: Brand, Marketing, Content, Data, AI, Organisation. A transatlantic fireside mid-morning between Destination Canada, Brand USA, and a European NTO. The day closes with a report from the Council.

**Atmosphere.** A serious room for serious work. Peer authority over outside expertise. Pace, but with breathing room. Not a tech conference; a destination marketing conference that takes AI seriously.

**Visual cues the design should consider.** Six themes is a structural rhythm the design can lean into. The transatlantic fireside is a visual moment of its own (three flags in one room, in effect). The closing Council report is the moment the journey of the event reconnects to Day One.

### Day Three. The Trends Workstream

**Thursday 04 December, full day. Members-only working day. Capped per table.**

A working day at which the Future Destination Trends 2027 report is built in the room. Members move between eight trend tables. Each table writes a position. Positions become the published report; contributors are named on every trend they shaped. Tables become quarterly working groups through 2027.

**Atmosphere.** Generative, forward-looking, almost optimistic. The most exciting day of the three. The day the next year gets written.

**Visual cues the design should consider.** Eight trend tables is a structural module that may invite a specific visual treatment. The proposition is privilege of membership; this should be legible. Hierarchies of contribution are part of the system (each member is named in the report against the trends they worked).

### The arc

The four moments (Council, Awards, Programme, Workstream) form an arc: a private start, a celebratory pivot, a public substantive day, a generative close. The design should make this arc visible to anyone navigating the site, ideally without explicit wayfinding labels. Where you are in the journey should be felt.

## 5. Audience by day

The brief deliberately does not collapse "destination leaders" into a single audience. Each room has a different person in it.

**Day One.** Tourism Ministers, CEOs and senior leadership teams of national tourism organisations and destination marketing and management organisations. Senior figures from the technology companies whose decisions shape the sector (drawn through Fáilte Ireland's Dublin relationships). One or two policy voices from the EU and member states. A small number of structured contributors including Oliver Csendes (former CEO, Visit Hungary; Destination AI Strategist).

**Day One Evening.** All Day One participants plus the wider DTTT membership in Dublin for the keynote programme. X. Awards entrants. Shortlisters and judges from the destination sector. The room is broader and looser than Day One but the recognition is serious.

**Day Two.** CMOs, marketing directors, brand leads, content leads, data leads, AI leads and senior digital team members from destinations and DMOs globally. Mostly DTTT members. Mostly senior, mostly active in the work.

**Day Three.** DTTT members specifically. The room is most often heads of strategy, heads of insight, heads of innovation, heads of brand, and senior marketers within member organisations. People who want to shape what the sector watches in 2027.

A meta point. Across all four moments, the visual register has to land for someone whose default reading list includes the Financial Times rather than industry trade press. That single observation excludes a large amount of category visual language by itself.

## 6. What is locked: the design system foundations

These are not open for the design phase to change. They are the brand book.

**Palette.**

- Cream `#F5F0E9` is the canvas. Used on roughly 95 percent of surfaces.
- Purple is the brand. Five steps: `#EDE5F8` `#C9AAEE` `#9B6FE0` `#8239C1` `#5B1FBF`. No second hue. If differentiation is needed, change the weight of purple or the opacity of ink.
- Greyscale ink for type: `#1A1A1A` `#3A3A3A` `#7A7A7A` `#B0B0B0`.
- Dark treatment (`fdb-dark`) is a selective alternate, not the default. On dark the purple shifts to a hot magenta-pink `#E070F0`.

**Typeface.** Geist, all nine weights. Self-hosted (TTFs supplied). Substitution is not allowed. Hero titles are 800 with `-0.035em` tracking and near-1.0 line height, wrapping into three to four short lines on purpose. Body is 400. Micro-labels are 700 UPPERCASE with 0.10 to 0.14em tracking and do the structural work on the page.

**The mark.** The stacked-square motif at three scales:
- 20 px in the footer
- 30 px next to the wordmark in the header
- 120 to 180 px as a hero decoration
- 200+ px as decorative outlines bleeding off the page edge in social frames

**The X. Awards lockup.** Canonical assets exist at `assets/logos/x-awards-wordmark.png` and `assets/logos/x-awards-mark.png`. The X. Awards is a sub-brand of FDB using the same brand purple. The italic *x.* glyph and the stack-square frame are its signature. Do not redraw, recolour or recompose.

**Spacing.** 1000 px content max-width. Generous vertical rhythm. Spacing scale on 4 and 8.

**Borders and radii.** Hairline borders. Small radii (2 to 6 px). No fully rounded shapes anywhere. Almost no shadows.

**Animation.** Minimal. 0.15s colour transitions on hover. No transforms, no parallax, no entrance animation. The brand is still.

The design system lives at `https://design.thinkdigital.travel/fdb/` and ships as a skill at `/mnt/skills/user/fdb-design/`.

## 7. The design questions this phase needs to answer

These are the specific problems the design phase should resolve. They are framed as questions because they are open.

1. **How does each day look different while staying within one system?** The current pages are variations of one template. They should not be. What are the specific moves (accent weight, motif scale, layout rhythm, imagery, type treatment) that mark Day One as Day One and Day Three as Day Three?

2. **How does the stack-square motif flex across the event?** It is the brand's signature gesture and is currently used once per page in roughly the same way. Consider scale, opacity, intersection with type, placement across the page, possible use as a wayfinding system across the arc of the event.

3. **What is the role of imagery?** The brand book describes portraits (studio shots, desaturated blue-grey backdrop, purple-outlined frame with offset decorative frame behind) and location shots (cool, slightly desaturated, light purple cast). None of the current build uses imagery. What is the right way for it to enter the system? Speaker portraits? Venue shots? Both? Neither, with the system holding without imagery?

4. **What is the X. Awards sub-brand expression on its own page?** The canonical lockup exists but the page around it should feel like a sub-brand moment, not a sibling page. Consider whether this is the one moment of the event where the dark treatment is fully expressed.

5. **How does the journey through the event read visually?** Invitation, ceremony, public programme, working day. Is there a navigation cue, a scroll cue, a header cue, a progressively unfolding motif that makes the position in the arc legible?

6. **How are the published outputs (Declaration, Code, State of Destination AI Report, Executive Briefing) expressed visually?** These documents are part of the system; they are not afterthoughts. They will be read in Brussels and in national capitals. They deserve a treatment that signals that.

7. **What is the visual character of the Council formation moment?** The room constitutes a Council. This is the standing output of Day One. Currently it sits as a section header. It should feel like the moment it is.

8. **How does the trends workstream feel forward-looking and generative rather than serious and reportorial?** The brief Riva flagged was that "annual report launch" framing makes Day Three too serious for what should be the most generative day. The design should solve this where the copy has begun to.

9. **What does the registration moment look like?** A single Register button is the consistent right-side nav element across all six pages. The destination of that button is currently a placeholder anchor. The actual registration page or modal is part of the design phase.

10. **What is the visual relationship between FDB 2026 and the wider DTTT brand?** DTTT runs three flagship events: X. Design Week (XDW), CAMPUS, and FDB. Each has a distinct identity. The brand book describes XDW as the Destination AI Intensive (Brussels, June) and CAMPUS as sustainability and innovation (Turku Archipelago, Finland, September/October). FDB is the CMO summit. The three should not feel like the same event with different names. They should not feel like unrelated events either.

## 8. Constraints and non-negotiables

The design must hold all of the following.

- Cream is the canvas. Dark is selective, not default.
- One typeface (Geist). One accent (brand purple, in five weights). No second hue.
- No emoji, no gradients, no drop shadows, no fully rounded shapes (the X. Awards roundel is the only circle in the system).
- British English throughout. No em dashes. No exclamation marks.
- Sentence case for headings, often ending in a full stop ("Brand."). UPPERCASE eyebrows with wide tracking for meta labels.
- Inline emphasis is bold and purple on a 2 to 5 word fragment, one per paragraph at most. Italic is reserved for TBC placeholders and the X. Awards *x.* glyph.
- The middle-dot `·` is the canonical separator.
- Fáilte Ireland co-branding lives in the footer on every page and in a partner row inside the home page hero only. The FI logo must be used unaltered.
- The right-side navigation element across all pages is a single fixed-width Register button. No per-page pills, no width-jumping.
- The FDB lockup at 64 px in the 96 px sticky header, with "Dublin · December 2026" or equivalent meta beside it.
- The X. Awards canonical lockup is used unaltered wherever the sub-brand appears.

## 9. Voice and copy

The copy in the existing pages is a working draft in the FDB editorial voice. It is in reasonable shape and probably does not need wholesale rewriting. The design phase should feel free to revisit individual lines where a design move requires it, but should not feel obliged to start from scratch.

If the copy is revised, the voice rules in the brand book hold:

- Editorial, restrained, declarative. Reads like a private memo to a knowledgeable peer.
- Short sentences. Periods carry weight.
- No first-person plural ("we") except sparingly. Name the sector directly ("the sector", "the room", "destinations").
- No imperatives ("Sign up today"). Replace with state ("Places are by personal invitation only").
- One purple bold inline emphasis per paragraph maximum.
- British English throughout.

Sample lines the brand book holds up as canonical:

- "The question is **not whether to engage.** It is whether leadership is ready."
- "The format is a meeting, not a conference. Boardroom arrangement, no stage, no panels, no commercial agenda."
- "What participants bring to this table does not stay in the room."
- "Places are by personal invitation only. To discuss participation, please get in touch."

## 11. What this brief is not

It is not a wireframe. It does not specify layouts.

It is not a copy brief. The working copy exists.

It is not an exhaustive content brief. The structural shape of each page exists in the working build.

It is the brief for what is missing: the visual language, the design moves that distinguish the three days, the imagery decisions, the journey treatment, and the moments (Council formation, X. Awards, the published outputs) that deserve more than a section heading.

The goal is an event that, when designed properly, is the kind of event the people in the room remember and return to. The brief from Riva was: genuinely outstanding, not merely good.

That is the bar.
